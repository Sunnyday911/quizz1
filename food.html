Local food

Here are top 4 places that you must gor while you are in sidoarjo

Kue Lumpur Bu Lilik

Kue lumpur is a traditional Indonesian cake known for its soft and moist texture. The name “kue lumpur” translates to “mud cake,” but don’t let that fool you. It’s a delicious treat! This cake is typically made from a mixture of mashed potatoes, flour, sugar, eggs, and coconut milk. The batter is often flavored with vanilla and sometimes pandan, giving it a fragrant aroma. The cake is usually cooked in small, round molds and topped with raisins or slices of young coconut. The result is a rich, creamy cake with a slightly custard-like consistency. Kue lumpur is a popular snack and is often enjoyed with a cup of tea or coffee.

Rawon Gajah Mada

Rawon Gajah Mada is a well-known culinary spot in Sidoarjo, East Java, famous for its delicious rawon, a traditional Indonesian beef soup. Located on Jalan Gajah Mada, this eatery has become a favorite among locals and tourists alike. The restaurant is praised for maintaining a consistent taste over the years, making it a go-to spot for rawon lovers. It’s also known for its late-night hours, staying open from 10:00 AM to 3:00 AM, making it a popular choice for night owls looking for a hearty meal

Lontog Kupang Pak Eko

Lontong Kupang is a traditional dish from Sidoarjo, East Java, Indonesia. It features lontong, which are compressed rice cakes, served with kupang, tiny clams, and a savory broth. The dish also includes lentho, a type of fritter made from grated cassava, tolo beans, and grated coconut, adding a crunchy texture. Petis, a thick, dark shrimp paste, adds a rich umami flavor. The broth, made from the cooking liquid of the kupang, is often seasoned with garlic, chili, and other spices. The dish is typically served with a generous portion of lontong, topped with kupang, lentho, and a drizzle of petis. The broth is poured over the top, and it’s often garnished with fried shallots and a squeeze of lime for added freshness.

Sate Kerang

Sate Kerang is a traditional Indonesian dish originating from Surabaya, East Java. It consists of marinated mussels that are placed on bamboo skewers and grilled to perfection. The marinade typically includes shallots, garlic, candlenuts, turmeric, ginger, coriander, grated coconut, chili peppers, oil, lime leaves, lemongrass, brown sugar, sweet soy sauce, and salt1. About five or six mussels are then placed on bamboo skewers and grilled until golden brown. Once done, sate kerang is often served with spicy soy sauce on the side
